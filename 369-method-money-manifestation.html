The 369 Method for Money: Tesla's Manifestation Technique for Wealth

A complete guide to the 369 method for money manifestation — where it came from, how to do it step by step, how to write affirmations that actually feel real, and the honest timeline of what to expect.

What Is the 369 Method?

The 369 method is a manifestation technique where you write a single affirmation three times in the morning, six times in the afternoon, and nine times before bed. You do this every day for a set period — most commonly 33 days. The idea is that this specific repetition pattern programs your subconscious mind to focus on and attract whatever you are affirming.

The method gained popularity on TikTok around 2020, but the numbers themselves go back much further. Nikola Tesla — the inventor and electrical engineer — was famously obsessed with the numbers 3, 6, and 9. He once said, "If you only knew the magnificence of the 3, 6, and 9, then you would have the key to the universe." Tesla used multiples of three in his calculations. He reportedly walked around a building three times before entering. He could not explain exactly why these numbers mattered, only that they kept showing up in patterns of nature and mathematics.

Whether Tesla's fixation on 3, 6, and 9 has anything to do with writing money affirmations is debatable. The connection is more numerological than scientific. But the numbers 3, 6, and 9 do appear in patterns throughout nature — spirals in sunflowers follow sequences related to these numbers, and they show up in sacred geometry across ancient cultures. The 369 method borrows that mystique and applies it to a simple daily writing practice.

Here is what I think matters more than the origin story: the method works as a focus tool. When you write the same affirmation 18 times a day, you are spending roughly 10-15 minutes with your attention fixed on a specific financial goal. Over 33 days, that is over 8 hours of focused intention. Whether or not the numbers themselves carry power, the consistency and focus definitely change how you think about money.

How to Do the 369 Method for Money

The process is straightforward. I will walk you through it step by step so you can start today.

Step 1: Choose Your Affirmation

Pick one specific money affirmation that feels true — or at least close to true — right now. This is the part most people get wrong. They choose something that sounds impressive but feels hollow when they write it. If your bank account is overdrawn and you write "I am a millionaire," your subconscious will reject it immediately. Start with something that stretches you without triggering a full-body cringe. I will cover how to write these properly in the next section.

Step 2: Write It 3 Times in the Morning

When you wake up — before checking your phone, before scrolling social media, before any input from the outside world — sit down and write your affirmation 3 times. Use a physical notebook, not a phone or computer. The act of handwriting activates different neural pathways than typing. Write slowly and deliberately. Feel each word as you put it on paper. This takes about 2-3 minutes.

Step 3: Write It 6 Times in the Afternoon

Around midday, find a quiet moment and write the same affirmation 6 times. This is the bridge between morning intention and evening reinforcement. Some people do this during lunch. Others step away from their desk for five minutes. The timing does not need to be exact — you just need to break the pattern of your regular day and redirect your focus back to your goal. This takes about 4-5 minutes.

Step 4: Write It 9 Times Before Bed

The evening session is the longest and arguably the most important. Write your affirmation 9 times with full presence. This is when your subconscious mind is most receptive to new programming — you are about to enter sleep, and whatever occupies your mind in the last 30 minutes before bed tends to sink deeper into your psyche. Some people pair this with visualization, picturing their financial goal as already complete. This takes about 6-8 minutes.

Step 5: Do Not Skip Days

The method asks for 33 consecutive days. Not 33 days with some gaps. Not "most days." Every single day for 33 days. Missing a day does not erase your progress, but it does break the momentum. If you miss a day, restart the count. This is not punishment — it is discipline. The point is to build a habit so strong that by day 33, the practice feels like brushing your teeth.

Writing the Perfect Money Affirmation

The affirmation is the engine of the entire method. A bad affirmation produces bad results no matter how many times you write it. Here are the rules I follow when crafting money affirmations:

Write in present tense. "I have" not "I will have." "Money flows to me" not "Money will flow to me." Your subconscious does not process future tense well. It responds to what feels like a current reality. When you write "I am earning $10,000 per month," your brain starts looking for evidence of that reality — and eventually starts creating it through changed behavior.

Be specific but believable. "I am earning $8,500 per month" is better than "I am rich." Specificity gives your brain a target. But if $8,500 feels impossible right now, try "I am consistently earning $5,000 per month and growing." The sweet spot is between your current reality and your stretch goal. You want it to feel like a natural next step, not a fantasy.

Include emotion. The best affirmations carry feeling. "I am grateful for the money that flows into my life easily" has more emotional weight than "I have money." When you write with genuine gratitude or excitement, you engage your limbic system — the part of your brain that drives motivation and behavior. Emotion is the fuel.

Keep it positive. Do not write about what you do not want. "I am no longer broke" focuses on being broke. "I am building wealth that supports my family" focuses on the positive outcome. Your brain processes images and words literally. It does not do well with negations — when you say "I am not stressed," your brain pictures stress first, then tries to negate it. Always state what you want, not what you are avoiding.

The 33-Day Challenge

Why 33 days? In numerology, 33 is considered a "master number" associated with spiritual growth, compassion, and transformation. The number 33 reduces to 6 (3+3), which is one of the core numbers in Tesla's sequence. Some manifestation teachers claim 33 days is the minimum time needed to rewire a habitual thought pattern in the brain.

I do not know if the numerological explanation holds up. What I do know is that 33 days is long enough to build a real habit and short enough that most people can commit to it. Research on habit formation from University College London found that it takes an average of 66 days for a behavior to become automatic — but noticeable changes in how you feel about the behavior start around day 30.

Here is a realistic day-by-day breakdown of what most people experience during the 33-day challenge:

Days 1-5: The Excitement Phase. Everything feels fresh. You are motivated. The writing feels purposeful. You might even feel a buzz of energy after each session. This is normal — it is the novelty effect. Enjoy it but do not rely on it. It fades.

Days 6-12: The Resistance Phase. The novelty wears off. Writing the same affirmation for the sixth day in a row starts to feel tedious. You might wonder if you are wasting your time. You might miss a morning session and tell yourself you will "catch up later." This is the phase where most people quit. Push through it. The resistance is actually a sign that the practice is reaching your subconscious mind — which does not change easily.

Days 13-20: The Shift Phase. Something starts to change. You might notice your internal dialogue shifting — you catch yourself thinking about money differently, or you realize you are less anxious about a bill that would have ruined your week last month. Some people report noticing small financial shifts during this period: finding money, receiving unexpected payments, or getting a favorable price on something they needed. These are not guaranteed, but they are commonly reported.

Days 21-27: The Integration Phase. The practice feels like part of your identity rather than a task. You write naturally, almost without thinking about it. Your thoughts about money have shifted at a level that is hard to articulate. You might not even realize the shift until someone comments on how differently you are acting around finances.

Days 28-33: The Completion Phase. You finish strong. The last few days carry a sense of accomplishment. By day 33, you have spent over 8 hours writing about one specific financial goal. That level of focus changes people — regardless of whether the numbers 3, 6, and 9 have any mystical properties.

Common Mistakes People Make with the 369 Method

I have read through hundreds of forum posts and social media threads about the 369 method. The mistakes people make fall into a few clear patterns:

Using future tense. "I will become wealthy" or "I am going to earn more money" sounds positive, but it keeps your goal in the future — always arriving, never present. Your subconscious responds to what you tell it is happening now. Change "I will" to "I am." The shift is small but significant.

Being too vague. "I am rich" or "I have abundance" does not give your brain enough to work with. Your reticular activating system — the part of your brain that filters what you notice — needs specific targets. "I am earning $6,000 per month" gives it something concrete to look for. Vague affirmations produce vague results.

Skipping days. This is the number one reason people say the method did not work for them. They skip a day here, miss a session there, and then blame the method. Skipping resets the momentum. If you miss a morning session, do not skip it entirely — do it when you remember. But if you miss a full day, most practitioners recommend starting the count over.

Changing affirmations too often. You wrote "I am earning $5,000 per month" for three days, then switched to "I am debt-free," then changed again to "I am financially free." Your subconscious needs repetition to accept a new belief. Pick one affirmation and stick with it for the full 33 days. You can start a new round with a different affirmation afterward.

Writing mechanically without feeling. If you are scribbling your affirmation 18 times a day while thinking about lunch, you are doing busywork, not manifestation. The writing needs presence. You do not have to feel ecstatic every session, but you need to be mentally present with the words. If your mind wanders, pause, take a breath, and re-read what you just wrote before continuing.

Expecting overnight results. The 369 method is not a lottery ticket. It is a daily practice that gradually shifts your focus, beliefs, and behavior around money. If you are looking for a $10,000 check to appear in your mailbox by day 5, you will be disappointed. If you are open to subtle shifts in how you think and act around money — shifts that compound over weeks — the method has a better chance of delivering real changes.

Many people report shifts in their financial reality after practicing the method consistently for 33 days. Most people who stick with it report noticing internal shifts between days 8 and 21 — reduced money anxiety, noticing new opportunities, feeling more confident about financial decisions. Visible financial results vary widely. Some people see changes within the first two weeks. Others take 30-90 days or longer. The internal shifts usually come first. External results tend to follow. Can I change my affirmation during the 33 days? +

Technically you can do whatever you want — nobody is policing your notebook. But changing your affirmation mid-way defeats the purpose. The repetition is what programs your subconscious. If you keep switching targets, your brain never fully absorbs any single one. Pick one affirmation and commit to it for the full 33 days. After the cycle is complete, you can start a new round with a different affirmation if you want to.

Do I need to write by hand or can I type it? +

Handwriting is strongly recommended. Research in cognitive science shows that handwriting engages more areas of the brain than typing — including regions linked to memory, language processing, and emotional encoding. The slower pace of writing also forces you to be more deliberate with each word. If handwriting is genuinely not possible for you (severe arthritis, for example), typing is better than not doing it at all. But if you can write by hand, do.
